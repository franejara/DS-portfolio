Forecasting Rent Growth in America's Most Walkable Cities: A Time Series Modeling Approach

A passion project

A year ago, my partner and I relocated to a different neighborhood when our rent increased by over $300. This sudden increase came as a shock to us, as we had not experienced such a significant hike in the past six years. This made me curious about the situation in other cities across the country. Were they facing a similar trend? And if so, which cities were being hit the hardest? Furthermore, I wondered if there were any cities that were both urban and walkable while still maintaining reasonable rent prices.

Finding the right dataset

After clarifying the aim of my project, I began searching for relevant data sources. While I came across several well-known databases containing rental price information, I found one database particularly well-done for a project like this, allowing me to start using it immediately. It contained a comprehensive time series of the percentage growth of monthly rent prices in 130 U.S. cities over the last five years. I found it on apartmentlist.com.

I chose the ranking system by walkscore.com to identify the most walkable cities, which I then selected for this time series modeling project.

Models, techniques and libraries used for this project:

  • Python libraries used: NumPy, Pandas, Matplotlib, Statsmodels and Sklearn.
  • Initial EDA, selecting the cities for modeling, and transforming each of these subsets into univariate time series,
  • Decomposition of the time series, applying the Seasonal Decomposition of Time Series (STL) and implementing the Augmented Dicky-Fuller test,
  • Splitting the dataset for training, and differencing the training set.
  • Model evaluation: ARIMA.

Current stage of this project: work in progress

As mentioned, I am currently working on this project and have begun applying the initial models to the data. The Jupyter notebook I am using is still in the first draft phase and is filled with experimental and testing code.

Segmenting Customers Using Clustering Techniques: A Data Science Project Overview

The business challenge

After four email campaigns and no conversion, the marketing team realizes that this is a crucial project to focus on, specially now that are planning next year's marketing strategies. They had never invested in profiling their customer base, and they need to know what are the characteristics of the existing segments they are targeting.

To help them improve their engagement and conversion rate, I utilized clustering techniques to segment the customers based on a dataset they provided, that contained demographics, buying behavior, and interaction with previous email campaigns. The primary aim of this project was to assist them in understanding and defining their customer segments better, to develop effective marketing strategies.

Models, techniques and libraries used for this project:

  • EDA, including univariate and bivariate analyses, feature engineering and data processing.
  • Preparing data for segmentation: separating behavioural customer data from the columns that will be used for profiling (demographic data).
  • Preprocessing the data applying PCA and T-SNE.
  • Clustering techniques: K-Means, K-Medoids, Hierarchical clustering, DBSCAN, and Gaussian Mixture Model.
  • For clusters' assessment and evaluation: The Elbow method and Silhouette score.
  • Python libraries used: NumPy, Pandas, Sklearn, Matplotlib, Seaborn.

The proposed solution: four distinct customer segments

After experimenting with different clustering models and comparing their results not only from a technical perspective but also business-wise, I chose the K-Means clustering model using K=4 as the optimal solution.

This approach provided four distinct customer segments based on their purchasing behavior patterns and product preferences. The chosen solution was superior to other alternatives as it provided clear differentiation between customers, enabling effective targeting strategies - even when that meant not choosing the best models technically.

For example, there were a few models that had better silhouette scores than the model that I chose as the best solution, but at the moment of profiling their clusters, they were not differentiated in ways that were useful from a marketing standpoint. Some of them would put all the purchases of any product in one cluster, and none on the others. Or they wouldn't differentiate clearly regarding sales channels or the amount spent monthly in the store.

The solution I will present now gives the most distinguishable and meaningful differences between clusters.

The model: K-Means, for k=4

Diving deeper into each segment profile

1st Segment:

  • They are 1,003 low-income customers.
  • Buy the least of all segments in any product.
  • Purchase mainly store.
  • Low price purchases.
  • Have not accepted any email marketing campaign.

2nd Segment:

  • They are 468 medium-high income customers.
  • Buy highest quantities of fish, fruits, sweets, and gold products of all segments.
  • largest total purchases, and second highest purchase value.
  • Have not accepted any email marketing campaign.

3rd Segment:

  • They are 558 medium-income customers.
  • Wine is what they purchase the most.
  • They purchase deals more than all the other segments.
  • They purchase mainly in store and some online purchases.
  • Have not accepted any email marketing campaign.

4th Segment:

  • They are 198 high-income customers.
  • They purchase more wine than all the other segments.
  • They purchase mainly through catalog, followed by in store purchases.
  • They make the highest purchase value of all segments.
  • They have accepted one email marketing campaign.

Reasons for choosing this segmentation

  • It assigns products to different segments. This allows to target more effectively. On the contrary, none of the other models presented balanced segments regarding product consumption or sales channel preferred by the customers.
  • The number of customers on each segment is distributed in a fairly balanced proportion.
  • The differences between segments profiles, provides valuable insights that can be effectively leveraged for marketing strategies.

Recommendations and action steps

  • Test this customer segmentation model for a defined period to measure the impact on conversion rates. By monitoring the results and collecting this data, we can evaluate further and identify any areas for improvement.
  • Continue gathering data, with a specific focus on tracking changes in customer behavior related to product purchases, sales channels, and spending patterns. By this we can refine the segmentation strategy and optimize marketing initiatives for each segment.
  • Track recency metrics as part of ongoing data analysis to improve customer engagement and customer loyalty strategies.
  • Define a deadline to apply the segmentation model again to the updated dataset. This will allow us to evaluate this solution over time, identify any shifts in customer behavior, and make any necessary adjustments to our approach.

Benefits to expect from using this segmentation

  • Improving the conversion rate of email marketing campaigns,
  • effective upselling and cross-selling,
  • better ROI,
  • developing stronger strategies to reach new customers, and
  • leveraging this input to redesign and improve sales channels and marketing initiatives.

Of course, there are always risks involved, and this solution is a recommendation that is yet to be tested on production. If after a period of time the marketing do not see changes on the conversion rate or ther customer behaviours, we can reassess the definition of these segments.
